MICROPLASTIC CONTAMINATION

  • Microplastics are tiny plastic particles that result from both commercial product development and the breakdown of larger plastics.


  • Microplastic particles ranging from 150 to 500 Âµm in size were the most predominant.A study shows that plastic debris is one of the dominant forms of marine litter and is estimated to reach 33 billion tonnes by 2050.


  • Once plastic debris enters the ocean, it degrades into smaller microplastic particles by photolytic, mechanical and biological degradation. These particles pose a risk to filter feeders, which often mistake the particles for plankton and consume them. Marine organisms from different trophic levels have been shown to ingest microplastics, and as a result, microplastic particles are estimated to have infiltrated the marine ecosystem.


  • Microplastics have the potential to carry anthropogenic contaminants, which are released into the digestive fluids of marine organisms and ultimately infiltrate their tissues. This has potential impacts on human health through the consumption of contaminated marine organisms.

    • Sources and Types of Marine Microplastics

  • Plastics are durable and cheap, leading to a rapid growth of the global plastics industry since the 1950s, with about 10% of waste plastics entering the ocean, accounting for 60-80% of marine waste, and even up to 90-95% in some areas. The main sources of microplastics in the ocean are land flow, coastal tourism, ship transportation, and fishing.


  • Microplastics in the ocean come from various plastic products and raw materials, such as polyurethane and polystyrene. The increasing demand for plastic products has dramatically increased the amount of plastics discharged into the ocean, leading to pollution and potential impacts on marine life and human health.


    • Distribution and Characteristics of Marine Microplastics

  • Microplastics are found in water bodies, beaches, and sediment, with high pollution levels in coastal waters due to human activity. The irregular shape and uneven surface of microplastics make them difficult to remove from the environment.


  • The characteristics of microplastics vary depending on their type, and they often attach to other materials and are subject to environmental forces such as ocean currents.



    • Influence of Microplastics on Marine Fishes

  • Marine microplastics can have toxic effects on fish and other aquatic life, including reducing food intake, delaying growth, causing oxidative damage and abnormal behavior. Nano-scale microplastics can penetrate biological barriers, accumulate in tissues, and affect life at the molecular level. Fish plays a key role in the marine ecosystem, and its health can affect the ecosystem's stability.


  • Moreover, marine pollutants can enter the human body by enriching in fish and threaten human health. Microplastics can inhibit growth and development, block the digestive tract, reduce energy reserves, and harm reproductive health. Polyvinyl chloride and polyethylene microplastics can cause oxidative damage and immunotoxicity. Research on the ecological effects of microplastics on fish is still limited but of great significance.



    • SOLUTION:

    Efficient removal of microplastics (MPs) from aquatic environments is critical in pollution control. Various technologies have been developed for MPs removal, such as coagulation, adsorption, magnetic separation, and membrane filtration, among others. Researchers have investigated the removal of MPs by water treatment technologies, such as coagulation, sand filtration, membrane filtration, and ozone oxidation. More than 90% of MPs can be removed through a series of water treatment processes. However, sustainability issues such as energy consumption and potential secondary pollution must also be considered.
