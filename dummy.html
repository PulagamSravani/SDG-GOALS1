OIL SPILL

To prepare for future oil spills, governments and the oil industry must have preparedness plans in place and conduct regular spill response training exercises. The speed and preparedness of the response can minimize environmental impact. The cleanup process involves mechanical means such as using booms and skimmers to contain and skim the oil, but manual clean-up campaigns are typically required when the oil reaches the shoreline. Natural recovery can play a role, but restoration efforts are crucial to help the environment recover. Before restoration can begin, an understanding of the damage must be undertaken through ecological, biological, and chemical studies and analysis. The importance of restoration after an oil spill cannot be overstated.

Efficient removal of microplastics (MPs) from aquatic environments is critical in pollution control. Various technologies have been developed for MPs removal, such as coagulation, adsorption, magnetic separation, and membrane filtration, among others. Researchers have investigated the removal of MPs by water treatment technologies, such as coagulation, sand filtration, membrane filtration, and ozone oxidation. More than 90% of MPs can be removed through a series of water treatment processes. However, sustainability issues such as energy consumption and potential secondary pollution must also be considered.
